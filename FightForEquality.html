All of the groups and people listed above played some role, big or small, in the fight for equality in Chicago alone!

Some of my favorite readings were early on in the course, readings like Political Education: Black Politics and Education Reform in Chicago since the 1960s by Elizabeth Todd Breland, or "Choosing a School for my Daughter," by Nikole Hannah-Jones.

I liked these readings in particular because they focused on stories and people that often go overlooked. To paraphrase the purpose of the Todd-Breland piece, black women organizing during the civil rights movement often did important behind the scenes intellectual work while being barred from leadership positions reserved for men.

If you take this course, you will hear about all of these untold stories and more, I even wrote a paper for another class that used Rosie Simpson's story as a sort of jumping off point. I never would have known about her work if it wasn't for this class. She was a single mother from Chicago who organized black parents against the clearly unjust, ill treatment of their children within Chicago Public Schools. She lead protests, issued important statements, she even had a face to face meeting with the Mayor. The mayor was trying to discredit her protests by saying they were "outside agitators," so she brought a petition signed by over a thousand parents to show him! She was kind of a badass and not enough people know about her, so it's up to you to take the course and tell your friends!
